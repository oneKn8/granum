# From Germinal Center to Granum — How Immunology Maps to Prompt Evolution

Granum is an immune system for medical appeals. The metaphor is not decoration. Every mechanism in the agent's tournament loop has a structural analog in the germinal center, the lymph-node microenvironment where B cells acquire high-affinity antibodies through repeated cycles of mutation and selection against a presented antigen. This document maps the immunology onto the engineering one-to-one, so that anyone reviewing the code can verify that the system implements affinity maturation rather than borrowing its vocabulary.

The mapping rests on three primary references: Mesin, Ersching & Victora's "Germinal Center B Cell Dynamics" review in *Immunity* (2016), Tas et al.'s "Visualizing antibody affinity maturation in germinal centers" in *Science* (2016), and Mayer et al.'s "The microanatomic segregation of selection by apoptosis in the germinal center" in *Science* (2018). The 2025 review by Pankhurst, "Highlights of 2024: Advances in Germinal Centers" in *Immunology & Cell Biology*, supplies recent updates on the apoptosis/selection balance that motivated Granum's negative-selection gate.

## The antigen, the B cell, and the appeal

In a germinal center, the antigen is a pathogen surface — a structured target presented to circulating B cells. In Granum, the antigen is a payer denial. Each denial carries the payer's medical-necessity criterion, the policy IDs invoked, the CPT and ICD-10 codes, and the specific documentation gaps the payer cites. A naive B cell is a low-affinity strategy that has not yet been refined against the specific antigen. A naive Granum strategy is a system prompt — a template for drafting an appeal — that has not yet been refined against the specific (payer × diagnosis) cell. The germinal center hosts a population of competing B-cell clones, each binding the same antigen with different affinities; Granum hosts a population of competing prompt versions in the Phoenix prompt registry, each producing different appeals for the same denial cell. Selection in both systems is competitive, not hierarchical: clones do not have a supervisor. They have rivals.

## Negative selection — the thymic-style gate before the tournament

Before a B cell ever participates in the germinal center, immature lymphocytes pass through negative selection in the thymus and bone marrow: cells reactive against self-antigens are deleted before they can cause autoimmune damage. In Granum, negative selection is implemented in `src/granum/center/negative_selection.py` as a citation-verification gate. Any candidate appeal that cites a policy ID not present in the cell's `valid_citations.json` is rejected before it can enter the tournament. The biological rationale matters: the LLM-as-judge problem is that two LLM-generated appeals citing equally plausible-sounding-but-hallucinated policies will produce LLM-judge agreement bias. The thymic gate excises that failure mode at the same developmental stage real immune systems excise self-reactive cells — pre-circulation. A hallucinated citation is the autoimmune-disease analog: a strategy that looks fit but would harm the host (the patient, the practitioner's credibility) if deployed. Negative selection is irreversible and absolute. It costs the system many candidates but produces a circulating population free of structural pathology.

## Clonal expansion — mutation as small, structured edits

Once a B cell encounters its antigen with sufficient affinity, it enters the dark zone of the germinal center, undergoes somatic hypermutation in the immunoglobulin variable region, and proliferates. The mutations are small, local, and structured: point mutations and short indels in CDR loops, not wholesale rewrites of the constant region. Granum mirrors this constraint exactly. The mutation operators in `src/granum/center/mutation.py` are a closed enum: `citation_swap`, `paragraph_reframe`, `guideline_change`, `evidence_reorder`. There is no `prompt_rewrite` operator. Wholesale regeneration would correspond to germline B-cell production, not affinity maturation — different process, different rate, different effect on diversity. The biological asymmetry between targeted hypermutation and untargeted lineage rebooting is structurally encoded in the agent: winners get mutated locally, not regenerated.

## Functional apoptosis — Phoenix prompt tombstones

In the germinal center light zone, B cells re-encounter antigen presented by follicular dendritic cells and compete for limited T follicular helper cell help. Cells that fail to capture sufficient help die by apoptosis. Mayer et al.'s 2018 paper showed that this apoptotic culling is microanatomically segregated and constitutes the primary mechanism of selection. Death is irreversible; the apoptotic cell does not retain a fallback option to revert. In Granum, functional apoptosis is implemented in the Phoenix prompt registry via the `tombstone` primitive in `src/granum/tools/phoenix_client.py`: losing prompt versions have the `production` tag removed and the `tombstoned` tag added. The Phoenix immutable-version-history constraint matters here. We cannot literally delete a prompt version — Phoenix preserves audit history — but we can mark a version structurally ineligible for selection or mutation. This is exactly the biological behavior: the apoptotic cell's DNA persists for some time in the tissue (phagocytic clearance is not instantaneous), but the cell is no longer in the selection pool. Granum's tombstone is functional apoptosis, not literal deletion, and that distinction is intentionally preserved both in code and in the demo framing. The audit trail is a feature: regulated medical-appeal use demands traceability, and lineage preservation is the immunological analog of the body's tolerance for transient apoptotic-cell debris before macrophage clearance.

## Immune memory — preserved champions for antigen reappearance

When a germinal center reaction resolves, a fraction of high-affinity B cells differentiate into long-lived memory B cells. These cells circulate quiescently until the same antigen re-emerges, at which point they expand rapidly without requiring a fresh germinal center reaction. Memory B cells are not active defaults — antibody secretion in the absence of antigen would be wasteful and potentially pathologic — but they are recoverable. Granum models immune memory by preserving champion prompts as `memory` tags in the Phoenix prompt registry, distinct from `production` (currently active) and `tombstoned` (apoptotic). When a denial pattern that has previously been overturned by a memory champion reappears, the memory champion can be reactivated for direct comparison against the current production strategy. This is the biological analog of secondary-response speed: a system that has seen an antigen before should not start from scratch.

## Antigen drift — periodic re-evaluation of champions

Pathogens evolve. Influenza's seasonal antigenic drift, SARS-CoV-2's variant emergence, and HIV's continuous diversification all illustrate the same problem: a champion antibody against last year's antigen may not bind this year's. The immune system's response is to re-engage germinal centers on each new exposure, re-running affinity maturation against the drifted antigen. Granum models antigen drift by periodically retesting old champions against newly added denial patterns. When payer policies change — Aetna updates a CPB, UnitedHealthcare modifies the Oncology Medication Clinical Coverage policy, Humana adjusts an MCP step-therapy rule — the cell's `denial_templates.json` and `valid_citations.json` are updated. The next tournament evaluates current champions against the new patterns, and a champion that no longer wins is demoted and the cell re-runs clonal expansion. The Pankhurst 2025 review's emphasis on the dynamic regulation of BCR signaling thresholds during the germinal center reaction reinforces this point: the threshold for selection is not static. Granum's antigen drift mechanism makes the system robust to a moving target without requiring human intervention.

## What the metaphor is not

Granum is not claiming biological fidelity to every molecular detail of germinal center biology. We do not simulate Tfh cell competition, chemokine gradients between light and dark zones, or the precise kinetics of somatic hypermutation. The mapping is structural: the same five mechanisms — negative selection, clonal expansion, functional apoptosis, immune memory, antigen drift — appear in both systems and serve the same function. Each one solves a specific failure mode that an unconstrained agent self-improvement loop would otherwise exhibit. Negative selection prevents hallucinated-citation contamination of the tournament. Clonal expansion prevents wholesale prompt regeneration that would dissolve gains. Functional apoptosis prevents zombie versions from competing with active strategies. Immune memory prevents repeated re-derivation of solutions already discovered. Antigen drift prevents drift-induced regression.

That the mapping happens to align with the project's verticals — medical appeals are themselves a form of immune response, a patient's documented evidence pressing against a payer's antigen-like denial — is not the bite of the project. The bite is that the mechanisms are real, the citations they evolve are real, and the constraints they enforce are visible in code and in the Phoenix prompt registry. The biology is the design vocabulary; the engineering is the deliverable.
